Problem

  • Negative wording;
  • Black background and diamond divider are associated with the VIP brand, not Cineplex Cinemas (A Cineplex branded piece would use a solid white line);
  • The logo used in this piece has the Rings scaled, and the ‘Cinemas’ written out in a typeface rather than using the proper logo. The typeface used is not Mark.

Solution

  • Ensure language places a positive spin on any given situation. Simply stating ‘NO’ to something should be avoided whenever possible. Aim to let people know the more inclusive ‘YES’ side of things. If the headline is restrictive, balance it out with an inclusive sentence below the divider line;
  • Mark should be used on all pieces;
  • Because a stanchion that calls out anage restriction is always related to anadults-only area, we know that this piece should have the darker/premium branding. The logo should be either ‘Cineplex’ (for a non-VIP Lounge) or the full ‘Cineplex VIP Cinemas presented by Scotiabank’ logo and diamond line (for any VIP space);

Problem

  • Too much copy – overwhelming;
  • There are areas for people to write in extra information – this should be avoided so that pieces don’t get extra messy;
  • While the intent for all of this copy is to be informative, longer explanations tend to come off as though we are making excuses, avoiding the issue/shifting the blame, and comes off as overly apologetic;
  • ‘Cineplex Employees’ should be referred to as ‘Cineplex Cast Members’;
  • The logo is missing;
  • The legal is missing;
  • The background is too dark;

Solution

  • Cut down on wording wherever possible so that we get to the point as quickly as possible. “Temporarily“ is assumed, and so we can remove it from the messaging;
  • In the body copy we keep the messaging clean and concise by letting guests know about the maintenance, we offer assistance, and we also thank people for their patience;
  • Because this is a functional information piece, we use the Cineplex logo, with a Consumer Blue background, and traditional Cineplex legal.

Language

TEXT SIZES NATIONWIDE

When advertising Nationwide, English comes first. The French can be equal in size or smaller, depending on the needs of the piece. Essentially it is the opposite approach as the Quebec market.

TEXT SIZES QUEBEC-ONLY

When advertizing in Quebec we must adhere to a 2:1 ratio where French takes prominence over English (headlines, body copy).

This rule is not necessarily applicable in all cases (ie. legal copy) as reasonable judgement must be used. A true 2:1 ratio would leave some text illegible if followed without question.

Rule of thumb, French must remain first and be more prominent.

Digital Screen Rotation

1:1 (EN:FR)

2:1 (FR:EN)--- twice as much time, or twice as many poster facings.

Wayfinding

Directional signage in theatres should be comprised of black type on a white background using Mark OT Medium, in all caps, as the primary typeface.

Information or icons may be separated by thick black lines - either vertical or horizontal (see example).

Social Media / Social Posts

Content should be more casual / fun, and direct resizes of movie poster or magazine ads should be avoided whenever possible.

Social posts should adhere to Facebook’s 20% rule - that is, text should only cover approximately 20% of the given image.
